A UI & UX Analysis of the South African Home Affairs Website

By Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted : April 21, 2025

Digital Exclusion by Design: An Ethical Analysis of the UI and UX in the South African Home Affairs Website

South Africa’s constitutional promise of accessible government services is fundamentally tied to the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), whose website https://www.dha.gov.za serves as the primary digital gateway for millions of citizens. Yet, for many, especially those from rural or marginalised backgrounds, this portal is not a bridge but a barrier, riddled with design flaws that reinforce digital exclusion.

This essay critically examines how the DHA website’s design choices unintentionally restrict access to vital services and information, particularly for underprivileged groups. While UI (User Interface) and UX (User Experience) are often seen as technical disciplines, they also hold ethical weight in shaping who gets access and who is left behind.

Drawing on principles from accessibility guidelines, design justice (Costanza-Chock, 2020), and UX best practices, this analysis identifies areas of improvement and explores how design decisions can better serve the full spectrum of South African citizens.

The Department of Home Affairs (DHA) is a key part of the South African government that handles essential services like issuing ID documents, passports, and registering births, marriages, and deaths.

The DHA website is meant to make these services easy to access online. Ideally, users should be able to find forms, understand procedures, and even book appointments from the website, saving time and reducing the need for in-person visits.

However, the website has problems that make it hard for many people to use, especially those with disabilities, older users, or people who are not familiar with the internet.

(UI) Visual Analysis: Clutter and Cognitive Overload :

Addressing clutter and cognitive overload requires focusing on core design principles such as visual hierarchy, contrast, alignment, proximity, and consistency.

To better understand the problem, I analysed a screenshot of the DHA homepage. The page is visually cluttered with numerous buttons, banners, and announcements stacked on top of each other with inconsistent spacing.

Good website design should reduce cognitive load by organising information clearly and prioritising the most important tasks. Users visiting the DHA website are likely looking to perform critical tasks like renewing an ID or finding visa information. However, the layout does not guide the user toward these actions in a clear or intuitive way.

Imagine a 16-year-old visiting the site to apply for an ID. Instead of being guided to a clear CTA (Call to Action) button, they are met with dense paragraphs of text, outdated design elements, and an overwhelming number of navigation links. This violates key UX heuristics like “visibility of system status” and “aesthetic and minimalist design.”

I tested the website using tools like the WAVE Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool and browser dev tools to simulate different screen sizes. Many key issues emerged: improper alt text on images, poor colour contrast, and text that doesn’t scale well for mobile or visually impaired users.

In summary, the DHA website’s cluttered visuals make it difficult to navigate, especially for users who are less tech-savvy or who rely on screen readers or assistive tech. The UI lacks focus and fails to prioritise user needs through a clear, clean layout.

(UX) (Ethics) Website Analysis: Accessibility Issues :

As the Institute for Internet and the Just Society explains, digital inclusion and exclusion remain urgent issues in South Africa. Over 25% of the population remains offline due to a lack of internet access, digital skills, or devices. A government site must prioritise inclusive design to bridge — not widen — these digital divides.

“The lack of internet-enabled devices and digital literacy,” both of which are closely linked to poverty, mean that websites like this must meet users where they are. When a site is designed with high data usage, dense content, or complex interactions, it excludes the very people it is meant to serve.

Language Accessibility: South Africa is a nation rich in linguistic diversity, with 11 official languages. Yet the site is only available in English, failing to accommodate a significant portion of the population who may be more comfortable in isiZulu, Afrikaans, Sesotho, or other languages. The lack of translation features creates an additional layer of exclusion.

Yuri Shapochka (2025) on UX Design for Crisis Situations regarding multilingual support stresses that government websites must support the public in times of urgency or vulnerability — like applying for birth certificates, death records, or immigration services. If the design isn’t built for those most in need, it’s not ethical.

The DHA’s language policy acknowledges the importance of multilingual communication and commits to promoting equitable access through all official languages (Department of Home Affairs, 2016). Yet, the website does not reflect this commitment in practice. Without language options or translation tools, the DHA website fails to serve a significant portion of the population, undermining the principle of inclusive service delivery in a multilingual society.

Internet Accessibility and Excessive Image Use: Excessive use of images on a website can make it difficult for users with limited internet access or expensive data plans to use the site effectively. Large or numerous images increase the overall page size, which leads to longer load times and higher data usage. This is especially problematic in South Africa, where many people rely on mobile data and have limited bandwidth. When websites are overloaded with decorative or unnecessary images, like the DHA is, users may struggle to access important information or may abandon the site altogether due to slow loading or high data costs. For public service websites like the DHA, this means many citizens, especially those in rural or low-income areas, are unfairly excluded from accessing essential services online, furthering the digital divide.

Based on my analysis of the DHA website, I have a few recommendations to improve the website. As designers, we need to create inclusive pieces that are widely accessible, based on the goals and needs of the people who will be using them. Aparna says, When we craft designs, it's crucial to ensure they resonate with a diverse audience. That's why accessibility considerations must be at the forefront from the very start of the design process. Prioritising accessibility makes our designs usable by everyone and creates a sense of belonging among users. It's about creating an inclusive environment where everyone feels valued and represented (2024 K.S. Aparna).

They need to establish a Clear Colour Palette. The website should use a consistent and limited colour palette that ensures strong contrast and easy readability. Avoiding too many distracting colours and maintaining uniformity will help users focus and navigate the site more effectively.

They also need to minimise image usage. Reducing the number of images, especially moving or decorative ones, will improve page load times and reduce data consumption. This is particularly important for users with limited internet access or expensive data plans.

To enhance the DHA website's intuitiveness, the DHA should adopt Peep Laja's user-centric approach: Go to your customers and observe them using the web in their natural habitat. You’ll get an idea of their current knowledge (Laja, 2022). This necessitates conducting in-person usability tests across diverse populations, including rural and urban users, to understand their navigation experiences firsthand. Furthermore, the DHA should integrate its online services with physical branches to reduce system downtime and improve reliability, focusing especially on establishing digital service points or online offices in underserved rural areas. By directly observing user interactions and creating accessible service points, the DHA can bridge the digital divide and tailor its website to meet the real-world needs of all South Africans.

In conclusion, the DHA website highlights how digital platforms can unintentionally reflect and reinforce existing societal inequalities. Its lack of accessibility, language inclusivity, and intuitive design reveals how marginalised communities are often left behind in public service digitisation. Ethical digital design in South Africa must go beyond technical efficiency; it must be radically human-centred. Future efforts should focus on regular accessibility audits, integration of multilingual support and optimisation for low-bandwidth environments. By embedding accessibility, transparency, and inclusivity into the design process from the start, we can create digital spaces that empower rather than exclude. For digital government services to be truly transformative, they must be shaped with and not just for the people they serve.
