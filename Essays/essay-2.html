The Internet, Geopolitics and Digital Coloniality

By Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted : June 12, 2025

Intersections of the Internet, Geopolitics, and Digital Coloniality: The Case of Meta’s Censorship of Pro-Palestinian Content

The internet, once celebrated for fostering community, accessibility, and global connectivity, has become a contested space where geopolitics, corporate interests, and digital coloniality intersect. The dominance of a handful of technology giants, primarily headquartered in the United States, has birthed a new form of coloniality. While not colonialism in the traditional sense, this digital coloniality involves the economic and informational exploitation of populations, particularly in the Global South, reminiscent of Cold War-era propaganda battles (Graham, 2014; Ballatore, Graham & Sen, 2017).

This essay critically examines these intersections through the lens of Meta’s systematic censorship of pro-Palestinian content during the 2023–2024 Gaza (ongoing) conflict. Drawing on academic literature, news reports, social media activism, and theoretical frameworks, this analysis situates Meta’s censorship within broader debates about digital sovereignty, algorithmic power, and the persistence of colonial logics in the digital age.

Theoretical Foundations:

Colonialism is defined as the policy or practice of acquiring full or partial political control over another country, occupying it with settlers, and exploiting it economically (Oxford English Dictionary, 2024). Digital coloniality extends these power relations into the digital realm, where data, infrastructure, and platforms are controlled by a few corporations and countries primarily in the Global North (Graham, 2014; Ballatore, Graham & Sen, 2017).

The Global North & Global South divide broadly distinguishes developed regions (such as the USA, Europe, and parts of Asia) from developing regions (such as Africa and Latin America), often reflecting historical patterns of wealth and power.

Couldry and Mejias (2019) introduce the concept of “data colonialism,” drawing explicit parallels with historical colonialism: “the appropriation of human life so that data can be continuously extracted from it for profit.” Just as colonial regimes extracted land and resources, data colonialism commodifies human life through relentless quantification.

Digital coloniality is not limited to infrastructure but extends to algorithms and moderation policies that determine content visibility, amplify certain voices, and silence others. Graham and Dittus (2020) argue that platforms such as Google Maps and Wikipedia, while serving as gateways to knowledge, are rife with gaps and biases that reflect and reinforce processes of exclusion.

Algorithmic colonialism, as articulated by Shakir et al. (2020), is marked by domination through invisible mechanisms, algorithms that perpetuate historical patterns of power and marginalisation. Algorithmic oppression privileges certain social groups at the expense of others, often encoding and perpetuating colonial biases through unrepresentative datasets.

Geopolitics and the Digital Ecosystem:

In the current digital era, geopolitics is defined not only by territorial disputes but also by competition for control over digital spaces, standards, and narratives. The rivalry between the United States and China over platforms such as TikTok and Huawei exemplifies how digital geopolitics can fragment the global internet, with profound implications for communication, trade, and governance (Nyabola, 2018).

For the Global South, this means navigating a digital ecosystem where access, representation, and agency are mediated and often restricted by actors far removed from local realities. The dominance of Western tech companies in Africa and elsewhere creates dependencies and power imbalances reminiscent of colonial rule (Nyabola, 2018).

This imbalance is not just theoretical but deeply felt in everyday digital experiences. For instance, my own social media feeds, such as the “For You” pages on Instagram and TikTok, are overwhelmingly populated by American, European, or UK voices, even when I am seeking content relevant to my local context. I frequently encounter content from American bakers, influencers, and commentators, while African or South African perspectives are comparatively scarce. When local or Global South issues do surface, they are often misinterpreted or overshadowed by dominant Western narratives. A recent example is the controversy surrounding Tyla and the term “Coloured”: in the US, the term is seen as a racial slur, whereas in South Africa it denotes a distinct ethnic identity. The global algorithmic privileging of Western perspectives often leads to misunderstandings, erasure, and the imposition of foreign frameworks onto local realities. This digital hierarchy not only shapes what stories are amplified but also who gets to define the terms of debate, reinforcing a sense of cultural superiority and further marginalising non-Western voices.

Case Study: Meta’s Censorship of Pro-Palestinian Content

Background and Event :

During the ongoing conflict in Gaza (from October 2023), Human Rights Watch (2023) and other organisations documented widespread, systematic censorship of pro-Palestinian content on Meta’s platforms, including Facebook and Instagram. According to a December 2023 HRW report, Meta’s moderation policies resulted in the removal, suppression, or shadow-banning of over 1,000 instances of peaceful content supporting Palestinian rights across 60 countries (Human Rights Watch, 2023; Mashable, 2023).

Forms of censorship mentioned included:

  • Removal of posts, stories, and comments raising awareness of the conflict
  • Suspension or permanent disabling of accounts advocating for Palestinian rights
  • Restrictions on engagement (e.g., liking, sharing, commenting)
  • Limitations on account discoverability and reach (“shadow banning”)
  • Temporary or permanent bans on features such as live streaming or monetisation, impeding fundraising efforts for humanitarian aid

Meta denied allegations of deliberate, systematic suppression, attributing the incidents to enforcement errors. However, the scale and consistency of the censorship, corroborated by multiple civil society organisations, suggest otherwise (Human Rights Watch, 2023; Mashable, 2023).

Social Media Activism and Resistance:

Despite these constraints, activists and ordinary users developed creative strategies to circumvent algorithmic censorship, such as employing alternative spellings, symbols (e.g., watermelon emojis, coloured hearts, and the Palestinian flag), and coded language.

A particularly notable example of digital resistance is the work of the creator Yuval (@yuvaltheterrible). Yuval employs innovative strategies to avoid algorithmic censorship on platforms like Instagram. He often begins his videos with unrelated or deliberately controversial topics to attract attention and engagement. Once viewers are invested, he shifts the discussion to issues such as Palestinian war crimes, encouraging audience interaction through comments related to the initial controversy. This approach helps his content avoid being flagged as pro-Palestinian and thus suppressed by platform algorithms. Beyond raising awareness, Yuval actively supports Palestinian families by fundraising to assist with relocation, food, and medical needs.

Yuval has also collaborated with other creators, such as Ayame (@ayame.p) and Oliver (@olivermillsn), to generate further engagement. For example, they constructed an ongoing narrative involving a fictional love triangle, which captured audience interest and drove traffic to their fundraising campaigns. The continuation of these storylines was linked to achieving specific fundraising goals, thereby leveraging social media dynamics to support humanitarian efforts for Palestinian families.

These acts of digital resistance underscore both the adaptability of marginalised communities and the pervasive reach of digital coloniality, where even resistance must be negotiated within the constraints imposed by distant corporate actors.

Analysis: Digital Coloniality and Geopolitical Power

Algorithmic Gatekeeping and the Coloniality of Power

Meta’s actions exemplify how digital coloniality operates at the intersection of corporate policy, economic interests, and geopolitical power. Content moderation practices on platforms like Facebook and Instagram are shaped not only by internal guidelines but also by external political and financial pressures. These dynamics disproportionately marginalise voices from the Global South and those critical of powerful allies, as seen in the systematic silencing of pro-Palestinian content. This is not merely a technical or algorithmic issue; it reflects broader power structures that privilege certain narratives while excluding others (Ballatore, Graham & Sen, 2017).

As Ballatore et al. (2017) argue, search engines and social platforms do not simply mirror reality; they actively construct it, privileging “localness” only when it aligns with dominant commercial and political interests. In this context, Palestinian voices become invisible on the global stage, reinforcing a digital status quo that mirrors historical patterns of colonial erasure.

The influence of corporate funding, sponsorship, and broader geopolitical alliances further complicates the landscape. Major technology companies are deeply embedded in global networks of investment and partnership, which can shape both overt and subtle aspects of content moderation. Analysts have noted that these relationships often result in the amplification of perspectives aligned with powerful stakeholders, while alternative or oppositional voices, such as those supporting Palestinian rights, are suppressed (Couldry & Mejias, 2019; Human Rights Watch, 2023).

It is important to recognise that while the backgrounds and beliefs of tech leaders may contribute to corporate culture, it is primarily the intersection of economic, political, and institutional factors that shapes platform policy. For example, public reporting has highlighted how lobbying, regulatory pressures, and international alliances can influence the moderation decisions of companies like Meta (Mashable, 2023). This systemic gatekeeping perpetuates digital coloniality by maintaining existing power imbalances and limiting the visibility of marginalised groups.

In summary, algorithmic gatekeeping on global platforms is not a neutral process. It is deeply entwined with historical and contemporary forms of power, shaping what is seen, heard, and ultimately remembered in the digital age.

Broader Implications and Paths Forward

Digital Sovereignty and Decolonial Futures

The case of Meta’s censorship is not isolated. Similar patterns of digital erasure and selective amplification have been observed globally, particularly in the suppression of protest movements and humanitarian crises in the Global South. For example, the ongoing conflict in the Democratic Republic of Congo and the aftermath of the Rwandan genocide have received minimal sustained attention in international media and on digital platforms, despite the scale of violence and exploitation involved (Nzongola-Ntalaja, 2013; Straus, 2006). The DRC continues to be exploited for its mineral resources, with little global awareness or intervention, an invisibility perpetuated by both traditional and digital media. This selective visibility raises urgent questions about digital sovereignty: who controls the digital public sphere, and whose stories are legitimised or erased?

Scholars and activists have proposed a few responses:

  • Developing local and regional digital infrastructures less dependent on Western platforms
  • Establishing stronger international governance and accountability for transnational tech corporations
  • Ensuring greater transparency and participatory oversight of algorithmic moderation and data practices
  • Supporting digital literacy and creative resistance among marginalised communities

As South Africans, it is crucial to amplify our voices on pressing political issues, such as gender-based violence and the ongoing legacy of apartheid, which are also subject to digital censorship. All communities deserve to be heard, understood, and empowered to vocalise their struggles.

Personal Motivation and Context

As a South African, the historical legacy of apartheid profoundly shapes my understanding of systemic oppression and censorship. Witnessing the ongoing conflict in Gaza, I am struck by the parallels between the suppression of marginalised voices there and the silencing tactics once employed under apartheid. Despite living in an era of unprecedented digital connectivity, access to truthful, uncensored information remains constrained. The suppression of content on media and social platforms perpetuates ignorance and apathy, echoing the ways international silence once enabled apartheid to persist. Just as global pressure and awareness eventually contributed to dismantling that regime, today we must advocate for transparency, platform accountability, and the amplification of voices facing injustice. Recognising these recurring patterns is crucial in resisting both historical and digital forms of coloniality.

Conclusion

The case of Meta’s censorship of pro-Palestinian content during the 2023–2024 Gaza conflict offers a powerful lens through which to understand the intersections of the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality. While digital platforms have promised openness and connection, they increasingly reproduce old hierarchies, privileging dominant narratives while silencing marginalised voices, particularly from the Global South. This is not accidental but rooted in systemic structures of economic power, political influence, and algorithmic control that echo historical forms of colonialism.

As digital citizens, we must question who controls our information spaces and for whose benefit. The struggle for digital justice is deeply connected to broader movements for decolonisation and equity. In recognising the power dynamics embedded in our digital lives, we can begin to advocate for more inclusive, accountable, and sovereign digital futures, ones where all communities, including Palestinians, South Africans, and other marginalised groups, have the right to speak, be heard, and shape the narratives that define them.
