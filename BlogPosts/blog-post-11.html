Week 14- Blog Post 11: My Approach to Justice and Ethical Practices in Development

Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted: May 26, 2025

How I Approach Justice and Ethics in My Artistic and Programming Practice

In my creative and programming journey, I always try to make my work authentic and true to myself, while also giving credit where it’s due to other creatives. I believe that acknowledging the contributions of others not only shows respect but also builds a stronger, more collaborative creative community.
But for me, the ethical side goes way beyond just giving credit. I’m especially passionate about using technology and design to help close the digital divide in South Africa and to create resources that can uplift communities, especially the youth, who face high unemployment rates.

Justice and Ethics: More Than Just Good Intentions

Justice in digital design, as Costanza-Chock (2020) argues in Design Justice, is about making sure that the tools and platforms we build don’t unintentionally exclude or disadvantage anyone—especially the most marginalised. This really hit home for me when I analysed the South African Department of Home Affairs website. The site, meant to be a gateway for essential services, is actually a barrier for many people due to poor design, lack of accessibility, and high data requirements.
I learned that ethical programming means thinking about who might be left out. For example, if a government website is only in English, or if it uses lots of images that make it slow to load on mobile data, it’s basically shutting out people who need it most. This is why, in my own projects, I try to prioritise accessibility and inclusivity from the start, things like multilingual support, low-data modes, and mobile-first design.

My App Idea: Tackling Youth Unemployment and Digital Exclusion

Inspired by these challenges, I want to build a mobile-friendly CV generator app for South Africans. The idea is simple: many young people, especially in rural or low-income areas, don’t have laptops or advanced digital skills, but they do have smartphones. With AI-driven recruitment becoming the norm, job seekers are often excluded just because their CVs aren’t formatted “correctly” for automated systems.

My app would:

  • Guide users step-by-step (in multiple languages) to create an AI-optimised CV
  • Work offline or with low data usage
  • Explain why certain keywords or formats matter
This project is about more than just technology, it’s about justice. As Aparna (2024) says, “Prioritising accessibility makes our designs usable by everyone and creates a sense of belonging among users.” I want my work to help level the playing field, so that talented young people aren’t left out just because of digital barriers.

Giving Credit and Building Community

I also believe in open collaboration. If I use someone else’s code, design, or idea, I always give credit. This not only respects their work, but also helps build a culture of sharing and learning, which is essential if we want to solve big challenges like youth unemployment and digital exclusion.

Final Thoughts

For me, justice and ethics in programming and art means:

  • Designing with empathy: Always asking, “Who might be left out?”
  • Building for real needs: Focusing on solutions that actually help people, not just what’s trendy or cool.
  • Sharing credit and knowledge: Lifting others up as I go.

By embedding these values into my practice, I hope to create work that doesn’t just look good or function well, but actually makes a positive impact, especially for young South Africans who deserve every opportunity to succeed.
