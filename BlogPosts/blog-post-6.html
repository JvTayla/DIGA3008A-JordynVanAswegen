Week 9: Research Internet, Society and Design Justice

Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted: April 21, 2025

How the Internet shapes our lives

Lately, I’ve been thinking a lot about how much the internet shapes our lives, and how unfair it is that it doesn’t shape everyone’s life equally. It’s easy to forget, especially when we’re used to streaming, Googling, or hopping onto Zoom without a second thought, that for many people, internet access is a luxury, not a given. The pandemic really opened my eyes to how deep this issue runs. It didn’t just push everything online; it exposed and intensified the digital divide.

One article that really stood out to me was by Tataki and Glynos. They unpack how the shift to online platforms for work, school, healthcare, and more during the pandemic ended up excluding entire communities, especially in poor and rural areas. The line that hit me the hardest was: “It is necessary to ensure that the most vulnerable are not left even further behind.” That stuck with me. Because while many of us adjusted to virtual classes or remote work, others couldn’t even participate. They didn’t have internet. Or devices. Or stable electricity. The playing field was never level, but the pandemic tilted it even more.

We can’t call ourselves an inclusive, fair society if whole communities are being left out of something as essential as digital access. The solution isn’t just about catching people up, it’s about making sure they were never excluded in the first place. We need real investment in infrastructure, yes, but also in free or affordable resources, education, and digital literacy, especially in underprivileged areas.

This brings me to another article I really resonated with, by Kasapis. They argue that internet access should be seen as a fundamental right. And honestly, I agree 100%. Whether or not we label it a human right, it’s clear that the internet has become the gateway to opportunity: to education, jobs, healthcare, connection, information. Kasapis puts it into perspective by pointing out that while billionaires like Jeff Bezos expand their online empires, “almost half of the world’s population does not have access to this digital social and economic network.”

That stat hit hard. And it makes me reflect on how much the internet has changed my life personally. I can learn whatever I want, from tutorials to university lectures, at the touch of a button. I can video call family across borders without worrying about airtime costs. I can stay informed, connected, and empowered. But it’s not fair that something that’s been so life-changing for me is still completely out of reach for so many. Digital access shouldn't be a privilege, it should be a standard.

Nanjira Sambuli, in her World Economic Forum interview, emphasizes that digital inequality isn’t just about where you live, it’s also about who you are. She highlights that people in rural areas, women, and those in lower economic brackets often face compounded barriers to accessing and using the internet meaningfully. Her point about how inequality exists both between and within geographies really stood out to me—urban vs rural, developing vs developed countries, and the gender gap that’s often overlooked.

She pushes us to look deeper, not just at the symptoms, but the root causes of digital inequality. And I love that she ties it back to the global Sustainable Development Goals, reminding us to “leave no one behind.” That phrase, to me, perfectly captures the heart of what digital equality should mean. It’s not just about laying down fiber cables, it’s about creating systems, policies, and cultures that empower everyone to participate in and benefit from the digital world.

So when we talk about bridging the digital divide, we’re really talking about social justice. We're talking about equity, opportunity, and fairness. The internet should be a bridge. We have the technology. We have the knowledge. What’s missing is the collective will to make digital access truly universal.

If we invest in infrastructure, yes, but also in education, accessibility, and digital inclusion programs, we can start to close that gap. And when we do, we aren’t just giving people internet. We’re giving them a lifeline. A platform. A future.

That’s the kind of world I want to live in. One where no one is left behind, where digital opportunities are open to everyone, regardless of where they live, what they earn, or who they are.
