Week 13: Ethical Internet

Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted: May 19, 2025

An Ethical Internet: Principles, Challenges, and Possibilities

An ethical internet, to me, is one that fosters responsibility, care, and respect among all its users, while ensuring that the digital environment remains inclusive, just, and conducive to the well-being of individuals and communities. Drawing on the work of Roger Silverstone and contemporary research on internet ethics, several key principles and challenges emerge that shape what an ethical internet should look like.

Responsibility and Proper Distance

Roger Silverstoneâ€™s concept of the internet as a moral space is foundational. He argues that the internet, as part of the broader media environment, can either contribute to the health or pollution of our digital world, depending on how its resources are used. Silverstone introduces the idea of "proper distance"-the ethical space between ourselves and others online. This means not just connecting with others, but also sustaining responsibility and care for those we encounter, even if they are strangers. True ethical engagement requires more than mere interaction; it demands that we recognize and respond to the needs and voices of others, especially those who are marginalized or distant from us (Orgad, 2007).

Empathy, Care, and Hospitality

An ethical internet is hospitable: it welcomes diverse voices and ensures that the "stranger should be heard". This involves creating online spaces where empathy, respect, and genuine listening prevail. The ability to "switch off" or disengage online can erode responsibility, making it easy to ignore the consequences of our actions on others. An ethical internet encourages users to resist this temptation and instead cultivate a sense of care for the well-being of others, recognizing the limits of anonymity and disembodiment that can sometimes undermine moral obligations (Orgad, 2007).

Truthfulness, Privacy, and Security

Ethical internet use is grounded in honesty, reliability, and the protection of privacy. Users should seek and share beneficial knowledge, verify information before sharing, and respect the privacy and dignity of others. This includes protecting personal data, complying with intellectual property rights, and avoiding harmful behaviors such as forgery, deception, or the spread of misinformation (Wang, Lin & Wang, 2025).

Freedom of Expression and Content Moderation

The internet is a powerful platform for free expression, but this freedom comes with ethical challenges. Hate speech, misinformation, and cyberbullying threaten the safety and dignity of individuals and communities, necessitating fair and transparent content moderation. Striking a balance between protecting users from harm and upholding freedom of expression is crucial. Content moderation policies should be developed through inclusive, multi-stakeholder processes that involve governments, civil society, and technology companies (Orgad, 2007).

Equity and the Digital Divide

An ethical internet must address the digital divide-the gap between those with access to digital technologies and those without. Ensuring equitable access to the internet is an ethical imperative, as digital exclusion exacerbates existing social and economic inequalities. Principles like net neutrality, which ensure equal treatment of all data, are essential to maintaining a level playing field online and preventing further marginalization of vulnerable groups (Wang, Lin & Wang, 2025).

Education and Ethical Awareness

Ethics education is vital for cultivating responsible online behavior. Teaching users about privacy, security, empathy, and the broader impact of their digital actions helps build a more ethical internet culture. Cyber ethics education promotes awareness, critical thinking, and the skills needed to navigate complex online environments with integrity and respect for others (Wang, Lin & Wang, 2025).

Conclusion

An ethical internet is not just about rules or technological fixes; it is about nurturing a culture of responsibility, care, and respect. It requires ongoing reflection on how our actions affect others, a commitment to truth and privacy, and collective efforts to ensure equity and justice in digital spaces. As Silverstone reminds us, the internet does not exist in isolation-it is part of a larger media environment that we all share and shape. Our challenge is to make this environment as healthy, inclusive, and ethical as possible (Orgad, 2007).
