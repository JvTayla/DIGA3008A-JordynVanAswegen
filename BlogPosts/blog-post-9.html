Week 12: My website through a Decolonial Lens

Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted: May 12, 2025

Reflecting on My Comic Book-Themed Website Through a Decolonial Lens

As a digital artist and game design student with a deep-rooted love for comic books, Spider-Man being a personal favourite, my website was intentionally crafted to reflect a sense of playfulness, visual storytelling, and dynamic interaction. The comic book theme, complete with page-turn effects and stylized navigation, does more than just express aesthetic preference; it represents my creative identity and artistic journey. However, as I continue to develop this site, I’ve become increasingly aware that web design, like all digital practices, exists within a broader historical and political context, one shaped by colonial legacies and global inequalities in digital access and infrastructure.

Decolonizing Web Development: Rethinking the "Neutral" Web

The internet is often assumed to be a neutral, universally accessible space, yet this perception overlooks the structural imbalances embedded in its design, infrastructure, and governance. As UNESCO points out, the digital sphere is shaped by economic, geopolitical, and cultural power dynamics, where the “master’s tools” cannot effectively dismantle the “master’s house” unless these systems are critically challenged. As a young coloured developer situated in the Global South, I find it essential to interrogate how my work participates in or resists these frameworks.

In building my site, I am mindful of how seemingly simple design choices, like layout, interactivity, and media use, can either reinforce or push back against dominant digital narratives. My goal is not only to build a visually compelling portfolio but also to engage with digital space in a way that reflects my cultural context and lived experience.

Balancing Visibility with Accessibility

One of the challenges I’ve faced is reconciling the need to showcase my digital and traditional artworks with the realities of limited internet access for many users. My site is inherently visual, which reflects the nature of my work. However, I recognize that image-heavy content can pose accessibility barriers, particularly in regions where high-speed internet or affordable data is not guaranteed, a direct consequence of ongoing digital colonialism.

This tension speaks to a broader challenge for creators in the Global South: how to balance the need for professional visibility with the ethical responsibility to make content accessible. To address this, I’ve focused on clean, responsive design and minimised unnecessary data consumption while still maintaining the integrity of my visual storytelling.

Interactivity and Experience: More Than Just Aesthetic Choices

The interactive elements of my website, such as JavaScript-powered page transitions, are designed to mimic the experience of flipping through a comic book, aligning with my artistic influences. These features help establish a stronger connection between the user and the content. However, their inclusion is not purely stylistic; it is guided by a conscious effort to enhance user engagement without overcomplicating the experience or excluding users with less powerful devices.

This approach is informed by ongoing research in interaction design, which emphasizes the importance of accessibility and contextual responsiveness. Inclusive design, in this sense, is not just about functionality, but also about ensuring that users from various socio-economic and geographic backgrounds are considered in the development process.

Cultural Identity in Web Design

As a South African developer of colour, my presence in the digital design space is inherently political. Western-centric aesthetics still dominate the web, shaping perceptions of what is considered "clean," "modern," or "professional." Through my work, I aim to challenge these norms by infusing my cultural background, creative inspirations, and local narratives into the site’s visual and structural design.

While my website does not focus specifically on Indigenous frameworks, I draw inspiration from decolonial methodologies that call for the centering of local knowledge, cultural specificity, and community-oriented values. This includes rethinking the logic of efficiency and minimalism often upheld in Western design and instead allowing space for expressive, playful, and story-rich interfaces.

Negotiating Between Artistry and Technical Constraints

The reality of building a creative portfolio online is that it must serve both artistic and professional purposes. While I want the site to authentically reflect my artistic identity, I am also aware that potential employers and collaborators may access it from varying digital environments. This makes technical considerations, such as load time, mobile responsiveness, and data usage, just as important as the visual components.

My design process, therefore, becomes a negotiation: between artistic integrity and accessibility, between personal expression and professional utility. These choices reflect a broader push within web development to move toward more equitable and culturally responsive digital practices.

Envisioning a Decolonial Digital Space

Ultimately, my comic book-themed website is both a personal and political project. It is a digital space that celebrates playfulness, artistic expression, and cultural identity while also engaging with critical questions around accessibility, representation, and power in the digital age.

By embedding decolonial thinking into my design practice, through aesthetic choices, user consideration, and reflective decision-making, I hope to contribute to a web that is more inclusive, nuanced, and representative of diverse voices. In doing so, I position my work not just as a portfolio, but as a small act of resistance and reimagination within the broader landscape of digital design.
