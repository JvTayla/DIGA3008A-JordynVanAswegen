Reflections on the Readings & the Internet Today:

Exploring the history and evolution of the internet through these readings has been fascinating:

  • "As We May Think" – Vannevar Bush
  • "The Library of Babel" – Jorge Luis Borges
  • "Flash and Its History on the Web"
  • "The Dotcom Bubble of the 2000s"

The Library of Babel:

In *The Library of Babel*, Borges presents the concept of an infinite library that contains every possible book, with every possible combination of letters, words, and punctuation. This means that within its endless shelves, one could find every book ever written, every book that could ever be written, and countless variations filled with slight differences. However, many of these books are pure gibberish, making it nearly impossible to find anything truly meaningful. This reflects the struggle of navigating overwhelming amounts of information, much like what we experience with the internet today.

The library represents both infinite knowledge and chaos, raising the question of whether ultimate truth is even attainable. Some inhabitants believe that somewhere within the endless volumes, there must exist a perfect book that explains everything... even the secrets of the universe. But with the infinite nature of the library, the search for such a book is nearly hopeless. This mirrors the real-world challenge of distinguishing valuable information from meaningless noise.

*The Library of Babel* can also be seen as a metaphor for the internet, where vast amounts of data are accessible, but not all of it is reliable or useful. Just as the library’s inhabitants must carefully sift through endless texts in search of meaning, modern internet users must critically evaluate information to find what is accurate and relevant. This idea extends beyond just information overload—it questions whether human knowledge is ever truly complete or if we are doomed to search endlessly without fully understanding everything. In the end, Borges suggests that true wisdom does not come from simply having access to infinite knowledge, but from knowing how to interpret and use it.

As We May Think:

Vannevar Bush’s *As We May Think* remains relevant even after 65 years, shaping the foundation of hypertext, information organisation, and ultimately, the internet. His vision of the “MEMEX”—a device designed to store and retrieve information based on associative trails rather than rigid hierarchical structures—was a precursor to the World Wide Web. However, while the internet embodies many of Bush’s ideas, it also highlights their limitations.

The MEMEX concept emphasised human cognition, suggesting that information should be retrieved in a way that mimics how we think through connections and associations rather than fixed categories. This principle is at the heart of the internet, yet paradoxically, we often struggle to find the information we need despite its vastness. The sheer volume of data available can lead to endless searching without reaching the answers we seek, revealing a gap between accessibility and understanding.

Bush believed that structuring knowledge based on association would enhance comprehension, but the internet has also exposed how fragmented and overwhelming information consumption can be. We are constantly navigating an ocean of data, yet we will never truly know everything, no matter how open access the web appears to be. This raises a deeper question—not just about how information is stored and retrieved, but how we consume knowledge itself. In a world where endless information is at our fingertips, are we truly learning, or are we simply accumulating disconnected fragments?

Bush’s work pushes us to reconsider not just how we organise information, but how we interact with it, reminding us that understanding is not just about access—it’s about making meaningful connections.

From this, I also wanted to consider how a smartphone would be in comparison to a MEMEX, and I found that the concept of the MEMEX and the modern-day cellphone share striking similarities in their roles as extensions of human memory and knowledge access, yet they diverge in execution and impact. Bush envisioned the MEMEX as a device that would allow users to store, retrieve, and link information through associative trails, much like the way the human mind works. Today’s smartphones function similarly, offering instant access to vast amounts of data, personalised knowledge organisation, and interconnected media.

While the MEMEX was designed to enhance deep, structured thinking by mimicking human cognition, smartphones often promote fragmented, algorithm-driven consumption, where information is curated for engagement rather than genuine comprehension. When we become too reliant on these algorithms, we risk being exposed only to content that aligns with our existing interests and beliefs, reinforcing biases rather than challenging them. Unlike the MEMEX, which encouraged user-created pathways of knowledge, smartphones depend heavily on external influences—social media algorithms, search engine rankings, and targeted content—that shape what we see, further narrowing our perspectives and limiting the breadth of knowledge we can access. In this way, while both devices function as tools for information, the MEMEX was designed to deepen understanding, whereas modern smartphones can overwhelm users with disconnected information, subtly shaping not just what we learn, but how we think.

Flash and its History on the Web:

The main thing I learned from this article and a bit of further research into Flash is that it was more than just a piece of software; it was a revolutionary force that shaped internet culture, fuelled creativity, and defined an era of web animation and interactivity. Flash’s history, from humble beginnings as FutureSplash to becoming the backbone of early online games, animations, and even video streaming, is a testament to its influence. The rise of HTML5 and the mobile revolution may have sealed its fate, but the loss of Flash feels like the loss of an entire creative era—a digital playground where ideas ran wild, unbound by rigid frameworks. It saddens me to know that so much content, so many groundbreaking experiments, and countless hours of creative labour are now lost to time, preserved only in scattered archives and fading memories. Flash’s legacy teaches us a valuable lesson: to challenge norms and innovate, but also to remain mindful of the fundamentals of the internet. It reminds us to create work that can be shared across multiple platforms, ensuring it’s not limited to a single ecosystem and can continue to thrive and evolve.

The Dotcom Bubble of the 2000s:

The Dot-Com Bubble was a period of intense excitement and speculation surrounding internet startups, many of which promised to revolutionise industries. These companies adopted a "Get Big Fast" approach, pouring excessive funds into marketing, branding, and customer acquisition, often selling products at a loss—all while securing massive capital investments due to the overwhelming enthusiasm for internet-based businesses.

One notable example was Priceline, a company founded in 1998 that allowed customers to name their own prices for airline tickets. The idea was simple: Priceline would sell unsold airline seats at discounted rates, benefiting both airlines (by filling empty seats) and customers (who paid lower prices). As a middleman, Priceline initially thrived. However, as its user base expanded, the company found itself operating at a loss—eventually having to pay full price for airline tickets while selling them at whatever the customer was willing to pay. While this strategy gained notoriety, it was ultimately unsustainable and led to significant financial losses.

For many startups during the Dot-Com Bubble, sustainability was an afterthought. Some companies took pride in being unprofitable, seeing it as proof that they were investing in future growth. However, once investor confidence dropped, the bubble burst—leaving behind bankrupt businesses and billions in losses. The Dot-Com Bubble stands as a cautionary tale about tech optimism and the importance of building sustainable models, not just flashy promises.

Another point that caught my attention was how, in the early days of the web, skilled developers were scarce. Companies struggled to find people with the expertise to build websites. Today, web development is everywhere, and we have no shortage of skilled professionals. But now, a new challenge has emerged—the rise of AI in development. Many tasks that once required human expertise are now automated, and while AI simplifies the process, it also means fewer people are actively learning and practising these skills. The value of hands-on coding and development is shifting, and we’re at a crossroads where AI is reshaping not just how we build websites, but also how we define expertise in the field.

My Goals for This Course

My main goal is to sharpen my coding skills and build a portfolio website that truly reflects my personal style and aesthetics (The Vibes). I’m particularly excited about working with HTML and JavaScript, as these skills will open new opportunities for me. I've been quite interested in web development since high school, so when I saw this when applying for university, I was intrigued. Additionally, I want to refine my design abilities, although I have been working with design since I was 15—around 6 years now. I'm hoping to deepen my skills and ensure that I not only understand best practices but can also execute them in final compositions. By the end of this course, I hope to have a well-designed, functional website and a deeper understanding of interactive media.
