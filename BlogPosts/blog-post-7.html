Week 9: Research Internet, Society and Design Justice

Jordyn Van Aswegen

Posted: April 21, 2025

Close Reading: Kasapis, S. (2020) ‘Internet Access is a Fundamental Right’

Introduction and Aims
Kasapis’s article, published in 2020 amidst the global COVID-19 pandemic, argues that internet access should be recognized as a fundamental human right. The timing of the publication is crucial: the pandemic underscored the internet’s role in maintaining social, educational, economic, and health-related activities when physical mobility was severely restricted. The article’s primary aim is to challenge traditional frameworks of rights that have not yet universally incorporated digital connectivity and to advocate for policy reforms that guarantee internet access for all.

Kasapis likely situates the argument within the broader discourse of digital rights and social justice, emphasizing how the pandemic exacerbated existing inequalities. By framing internet access as a fundamental right, the article seeks to elevate the issue beyond a mere utility or luxury to a necessity integral to exercising other rights (education, freedom of expression, access to information).

Methodology and Structure
While the article is a preprint and likely an argumentative essay rather than empirical research, Kasapis probably employs a normative analytical approach.

This involves:

  • Reviewing existing international human rights frameworks (e.g., Universal Declaration of Human Rights, International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights) to identify gaps related to digital access.
  • Analyzing pandemic-related data and case studies that illustrate the consequences of digital exclusion.
  • Engaging with legal, ethical, and social theory to justify the claim that internet access meets the criteria of a fundamental right.
Kasapis might also reference policy documents, reports from organizations like the UN or ITU, and recent legislative initiatives in various countries that have begun to recognize digital access as a right.

Key Arguments and Results

  • Internet as an Enabler of Other Rights: Kasapis likely argues that the internet is not an isolated right but a critical enabler of other fundamental rights, such as education, freedom of expression, and access to healthcare. During the pandemic, the shift to remote learning and telemedicine highlighted how lack of internet access effectively disenfranchised vulnerable populations.

  • Digital Divide and Social Inequality: The article probably details how socio-economic disparities translate into digital exclusion, disproportionately affecting marginalized groups-low-income families, rural communities, elderly populations, and people with disabilities. Kasapis may emphasize that without state intervention, market forces alone cannot ensure equitable access.
  • Legal and Ethical Justifications: Kasapis might draw on human rights law and ethical principles of justice and equality to argue that states have a positive obligation to guarantee internet access. This could include infrastructure development, affordable pricing, and digital literacy programs.
  • Policy Recommendations: The article likely concludes with calls for international recognition of internet access as a fundamental right, urging governments, international bodies, and civil society to collaborate on frameworks that ensure universal, affordable, and reliable connectivity.

    • Critical Reflection

    Kasapis’s argument is compelling and timely, especially given the pandemic’s role in accelerating digital transformation and exposing inequalities. By framing internet access as a fundamental right, the article aligns with a growing consensus among scholars, activists, and some governments.

    However, some critical points to consider:

    • Practical Implementation Challenges: While the normative argument is strong, the article may underplay the complexities of implementing universal internet access, especially in low-income countries with limited infrastructure. Future research could explore scalable models for connectivity that balance cost, sustainability, and inclusivity.
    • Privacy and Surveillance Concerns: The article’s focus on access might benefit from a deeper engagement with related digital rights issues such as data privacy, surveillance, and freedom from censorship. Access without protections can lead to new vulnerabilities.
    • Global vs. Local Perspectives: The article might generalize the internet’s role without fully accounting for cultural, political, and economic differences that shape digital rights in various contexts. Comparative studies could enrich the discussion.

    Relation to Additional Research

    Kasapis’s work resonates with other scholarship and policy initiatives:

    • The United Nations Human Rights Council’s 2016 resolution recognizing the importance of internet access for the realization of human rights.
    • Research by scholars like Rebecca MacKinnon and Susan Crawford, who explore the intersection of internet governance, rights, and social justice.
    • Studies on the digital divide during COVID-19, such as those published in journals like Information, Communication & Society, which document the pandemic’s impact on education and employment.


    Kasapis’s work echoes key voices and initiatives: the UNHRC 2016 resolution on digital rights; scholars like Rebecca MacKinnon and Susan Crawford; and COVID-era digital divide studies in journals like Information, Communication & Society. These reinforce the urgency and legitimacy of the argument.

    Suggestions for Future Research

    • Empirical studies on the impact of internet access interventions during and post-pandemic, assessing outcomes on education, health, and economic opportunities.
    • Policy analysis of national approaches to legislating internet access as a right, including successes and challenges.
    • Interdisciplinary research on digital rights frameworks that integrate access with privacy, security, and freedom from digital harms.
    • Exploration of community-led connectivity models (e.g., mesh networks, community ISPs) as alternatives to top-down infrastructure projects.

    Conclusion
    Kasapis’s article is a vital contribution to the discourse on digital rights, especially in the context of global crises like the COVID-19 pandemic. By advocating for internet access as a fundamental right, it challenges policymakers and society to rethink how digital connectivity is integrated into human rights frameworks. While normative in nature, the article opens important avenues for practical policy development and interdisciplinary research aimed at bridging the digital divide and ensuring equitable participation in the digital age.
